Key Terms You Need to Know

Parameter / Weight

A number the model adjusts during training. More parameters = more capacity to learn patterns. GPT-4 has ~1 trillion.

Token

The basic unit of text (~1 word). Models process and generate text token by token.

Loss

How wrong the model's predictions are. Training = minimizing loss. Lower loss = better predictions.

Transformer

The architecture behind modern AI. Uses "attention" to figure out which parts of input matter most.

Just enough vocabulary to follow the conversation. Parameters = capacity. Tokens = units. Loss = error. Transformer = architecture.

What is a Transformer?

A neural network architecture that processes sequences using attention

Attention Mechanism

"Which words in this sentence matter for understanding this word?"

Parallel Processing

Processes entire sequences at once, not word-by-word. Very fast on GPUs.

Layers of Refinement

Each layer builds more abstract understanding. Early layers: grammar. Later layers: meaning.

How Models Learn

1. Pretraining

Learn from vast amounts of text by predicting the next word

"Given 'The cat sat on the ___', predict 'mat'"

Trillions of examples. Learns grammar, facts, reasoning patterns.

2. Post-training (RLHF)

Fine-tune with human feedback to be helpful, harmless, honest

Humans rate responses. Model learns what humans prefer.

3. Inference

You ask a question, model generates response token by token

Each token is a prediction: "What word should come next?"

Pretraining = raw capability from data. Post-training = make it useful/safe. Key insight: It's all prediction. Not understanding, not thinking - prediction at massive scale.

The Core Insight

"AI is a prediction engine trained on human-generated data"

  • Not magic, not consciousness, not understanding
  • Pattern matching at unprecedented scale
  • It predicts what a helpful response would look like
  • The prediction is often very good because it's seen similar patterns millions of times

This is why context matters so much: better context â†’ better predictions

This demystifies AI. It's not thinking - it's predicting. But prediction at this scale can look remarkably like thinking. Frame for the rest of the talk: how do we use prediction engines well?

Theory of Mind

How to Work with AI

Section 3: Mental models for working with AI effectively. This is where we go from "what is AI" to "how do I use it well."

Communication is the New Bottleneck

With frontier models, output quality is limited by your communication clarity, not the model's capability

Old Problem (2023)

"The model didn't understand"

â†’

New Reality (2025)

"I didn't communicate clearly"

Better software engineering skills â†’ Better AI output (because you can articulate what you want)

THE key insight for working with modern AI. The bottleneck shifted. I rarely do more QA with AI than with human collaborators now.

AI as Peer / Collaborator

Treat AI like a brilliant colleague having their first day at your studio

They know:

  • General software engineering
  • Common patterns & best practices
  • How things usually work
  • Thousands of similar implementations

They don't know:

  • Your specific codebase
  • Your design constraints
  • Your project context
  • Why you made certain decisions
The "first day" metaphor is powerful. They're smart but lack context. You provide context, they provide expertise. It's a collaboration.

Ask What the Model Would Do

Instead of:

"Implement inventory system using these 5 data structures I specified"

Try:

"I need an inventory system for my roguelike. You've probably seen hundreds of implementations - what would you do here and why?"

Then: Verify assumptions, correct misconceptions, iterate

  • Models have seen thousands of implementations
  • They might suggest patterns you haven't considered
  • You can always override when they're missing context
This is incredibly powerful. Leverage their expertise. Don't just command - consult. Then evaluate and iterate.

Adopt Ambition & Agency

"It would be useful if we could do X.
AI, how can we do X?"

  • Many "impossible" tasks are actually scriptable
  • AI can often tell you HOW if you ask
  • Don't self-limit based on what you think is possible

Real example:

"I want to batch-convert 200 Unity prefabs to Godot scenes"

â†’ Model writes parser, converter, and validator in 15 minutes

Remove mental barriers. Students often think "that would take weeks" - try asking AI first. The cost of asking is near-zero. The upside can be enormous.

Keep Your Brain On

AI is a collaborator, not a replacement for thinking

  • Verify: Check AI's assumptions against your context
  • Understand: Don't ship code you can't explain
  • Iterate: First output is rarely final output
  • Own it: You're responsible for the result, not the AI

AI amplifies your capabilities. Make sure you have capabilities worth amplifying.

Critical balance. AI makes you powerful, but you need to steer. Don't abdicate thinking. The skill is in knowing what to ask and evaluating responses.

Broader Context

Ethics & Considerations

Section 5: Brief coverage of ethics and broader context. Goal: Help them think clearly, not tell them what to think.

Clear Thinking, Not Prescriptions

My role: Help you think clearly about tradeoffs

Not: Tell you what to believe

Approach:

  • Present multiple perspectives fairly
  • Explain arguments for and against
  • Give you tools to make informed decisions
  • Trust you to form your own position

You'll have to defend your choices in your career. Think them through now.

Set expectations: no preaching. You're adults, you can think. But you SHOULD think about this - not just follow the crowd in either direction.

AI Art: Multiple Perspectives

Concerns:

  • Trained on artists' work without compensation
  • Threatens artist livelihoods
  • Copyright questions unresolved
  • Devalues creative labor

Arguments for:

  • Democratizes creative tools
  • Artists can use it to work faster
  • Fair use / transformative work precedent
  • Enables new forms of expression

What's YOUR position? Can you defend it with clear reasoning?

Both sides have legitimate arguments. Don't pick for them. Key question: can they articulate WHY they believe what they believe?

Portfolio Guidance: Honesty

How to present AI-assisted work:

  • Be honest: Disclose AI use where significant
  • Explain your role: "I designed X, AI implemented Y, I integrated Z"
  • Highlight your contributions: What did YOU bring that AI couldn't?

Good:

"Built procedural generation system using AI-assisted implementation. Designed algorithms, AI wrote boilerplate, I optimized performance."

Avoid:

"Built complex procedural generation system" (with no mention AI did the implementation)

CRITICAL for job seekers. Employers will ask. Don't get caught lying. Your thinking/design is valuable even if AI wrote the code.
